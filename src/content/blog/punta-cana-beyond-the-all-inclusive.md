Let me be upfront: we stayed at an all-inclusive in Punta Cana. It was comfortable, the beach was genuinely beautiful, and the deal was hard to argue with. But we made a deliberate choice to treat it as a base rather than a destination, and that decision turned a perfectly pleasant trip into something actually memorable.

## Why the All-Inclusive Bubble Is a Trap

The math on all-inclusives is appealing — pay once, eat and drink freely, don't think about money for a week. The problem is that the model incentivizes you to never leave, and the DR has enough worth seeing that staying inside a resort fence for seven days is a genuine waste.

The resorts know this. They offer excursions at inflated prices, and some are worth paying. Many are not. Here's what we found by doing our homework.

## Scape Park: Skip Most of It, Do the Cave

The big tourist attraction in Punta Cana is Scape Park, a multi-activity park with zip lines, cenotes, and beach clubs. The resort excursion price was around $120/person for a package. We booked independently for $65/person and only paid for what we wanted.

The Indigenous Eyes Ecological Reserve — a series of freshwater lagoons within Scape Park — was genuinely worth it. The lagoons are crystal clear, fed by underground rivers, surrounded by forest. Swimming in them felt nothing like the beach. We spent two hours there and could have stayed longer.

The zip lines: fine, not exceptional. Skip if you've zip-lined elsewhere.

## The Local Beach Situation

Bávaro Beach in front of the resorts is legitimately world-class — fine white sand, calm turquoise water, predictable. But walk twenty minutes in either direction from the resort clusters and things change quickly.

We rented bikes one morning and rode north. Found a stretch of beach with a handful of local fishing boats, two small colmados selling cold Presidentes for $1.50, and maybe three other tourists. The water was the same water. The cost was zero.

## A Day Trip to Altos de Chavón

About an hour west of Punta Cana near La Romana sits Altos de Chavón — a replica 16th-century Mediterranean village built in the 1970s as an artists' colony for Gulf+Western Corporation, who owned the surrounding sugar operations. It sounds odd because it is. It's also unexpectedly beautiful and worth the half-day trip.

The amphitheater seats 5,000 and has hosted Frank Sinatra, Julio Iglesias, and more recently, major concerts. The archaeology museum is small but has a serious pre-Columbian collection. The views down into the Chavón River gorge are legitimately stunning.

Entrance is around $25. Hire a driver through your resort or negotiate a round-trip with a local driver for $60-80 total — much cheaper than the resort excursion.

## Food Outside the Fence

Dominican food is good and cheap when you eat where Dominicans eat. We snuck out twice for meals in the town of Bávaro (the actual town, not the resort strip).

**La Yola** at the Marina Bávaro — seafood with fresh catch, more expensive by local standards ($20-30/person) but delivering real quality. Waterfront setting, worth it for one splurge meal.

Street food: tostones (twice-fried plantains), chimichurri (Dominican burger), sancocho (slow-cooked meat stew) if you catch it at a colmado. Under $5 for a full meal. We had the best sancocho of the trip at a spot with no English signage, four plastic tables, and a woman who gestured enthusiastically at everything on the menu.

## The Honest Assessment

Punta Cana isn't going to change how you think about travel. But it's also not nothing — the beach is real, the water is warm, and if you push through the resort bubble even occasionally, you'll find a country that has more going on than the coconut drink delivery service suggests.

The DR rewards a little initiative. You don't need much.
